**Tech Stack:** PHP, jQuery, SQLite. Deploys in 1-2 seconds.

**Pricing:** $29/month for 1,000 generated images. Annual subscriptions at discount.

**Key Quote:** "Only 4 out of 70+ projects I ever did made money. >95% of everything I ever did failed."

## Cross-Cutting Playbooks

### Playbook 1: "Presell Before You Build"
**Founders:** Gil Hildebrand, Maor Shlomo

Validate with MONEY, not surveys. Gil presold $20K from 1,000 emails. Maor's first customer sent $100 unsolicited. Build an audience around analysis/insights BEFORE launching the product. If nobody will pay before the product exists, they probably won't pay after either.

### Playbook 2: "Boring Tech, Fast Ship"
**Founders:** Pieter Levels, Jon Yongfook, Gil Hildebrand

PHP/jQuery/SQLite (Pieter), Rails 6/jQuery (Jon), Laravel/single droplet (Gil). All reached $1M+ ARR on "outdated" tech. Jon's advice: "Use the same tech as your last project. Starting a new business is the worst time to learn new tech."

### Playbook 3: "Platform Ecosystem Leverage"
**Founders:** Erikas Malisauskas, Preetam Nath, Tony Dinh, Andy Cloke, Rick Blyth

Build where customers already shop (Shopify App Store, Airtable marketplace, Chrome Web Store, ChatGPT ecosystem). Zero marketing spend needed when the platform provides distribution. Erikas: $4.5M/year with $0 marketing budget. Andy: 70-80% of customers from Airtable marketplace alone.

### Playbook 4: "No-Code / AI-Assisted Speed"
**Founders:** David Bressler, Maor Shlomo, Louis Pereira

David built $220K/month with Bubble.io and zero coding. Louis built AudioPen in 12 hours on Bubble for $32. Maor used Cursor (AI wrote 90% of his code). You don't need to know how to code to build a million-dollar SaaS.

### Playbook 5: "Volume Over Perfection"
**Founders:** Pieter Levels, Max Artemov, Marc Lou

Pieter: 70+ projects, only 4 made money (but those 4 make $3M/year). Max: 30 apps in under a year after wasting 5 years on one. Marc: 28 startups before his breakthrough. Ship fast, let the market decide, double down on winners, abandon losers.

### Playbook 6: "Open Source as Distribution"
**Founders:** Nevo David, Adriaan van Rossum

Open source creates trust, community, and discovery channels. Nevo's Postiz: 26.5K GitHub stars = 4.79M Docker downloads = $14.2K MRR. Gets listed on curated directories (Awesome Self-hosted, etc.) for free ongoing traffic.

### Playbook 7: "Acquire, Don't Always Build"
**Founders:** Damon Chen

Bought PDF.ai for $20K, grew to $591K/year. Bought testimonials.io for $35K, made $80K from mistyped URLs alone. Premium domains + riding market trends = massive leverage. Sometimes the fastest path to revenue is buying an existing product.

### Playbook 8: "Free Tools -> Email List -> Paid Product"
**Founders:** Rick Blyth, Rashid Khasanov, Jon Yongfook

Build free tools that solve small problems for your target audience. Collect emails. Launch paid product to a warm audience. Rick's free Chrome extensions led to $500K+ in total earnings. Rashid built 8+ free tools as lead magnets. Jon's free generators became his top Google entry points.

### Playbook 9: "SEO-First Product Development"
**Founders:** Danny Postma, Rashid Khasanov

Don't build then figure out marketing. Research keywords FIRST (Ahrefs difficulty < 10-20), validate demand through search data, THEN build the product. Danny: "If people are already searching for it, they'll have budget for yours." Rashid: Programmatic SEO at scale (auto-generated investor profile pages).

### Playbook 10: "Community-First Distribution"
**Founders:** Aurelio Volle, Nevo David, Maor Shlomo

WordCamp sponsorships (Aurelio). Reddit /r/selfhosted (Nevo). LinkedIn community building (Maor). In-person and online community engagement creates trust that no ad spend can replicate. "Nothing beats human face-to-face interactions."

## Key Patterns Summary

1. **Speed of shipping beats quality of planning.** The founders who made money fastest (Louis, Maor, Yasser, Tony, Danny) all shipped MVPs in hours to weeks, not months.

2. **Distribution is the moat, not technology.** None use proprietary AI. All wrap existing APIs or build on existing platforms. Defensibility comes from brand, community, SEO, and user experience.

3. **Failed projects are building blocks.** Damon reused 80% of failed code. Marc built 28 failures before his hit. Pieter failed 70+ times. Tony's first project (6 months, 95% test coverage) was abandoned. Successful products launched with zero test cases.

4. **Boring tech wins.** $1M ARR on Rails 6 + jQuery (Jon). $3M/yr on PHP + jQuery + SQLite (Pieter). $220K MRR on Bubble.io (David). Nobody uses cutting-edge architecture.

5. **SEO and community outperform paid ads.** Louis wasted $7K on TikTok influencer marketing with zero results. Rashid ran Meta ads at 1.6x ROAS then paused them. Word-of-mouth, SEO, and community are the dominant growth channels.

6. **One-time pricing is gaining ground.** Danny, Marc, Rick, and Tony all favor one-time purchases for certain products. Reduces operational complexity and support burden.

7. **The "anti-VC" thesis is proven.** Maor owned 100% at $80M exit. Erikas makes $4.5M/yr with 90% margins and no investors. Gil argues a $1M profitable solo business outperforms a $50M VC-backed company in actual founder wealth.

## Non-SaaS Cross-Cutting Playbooks

### Playbook 11: "Chrome Extension = Near-Zero Costs + Near-100% Margins"
**Founders:** Jordan O'Connor, Guilherme Rizzo, Edmund Yong, Rick Blyth (#18)

Chrome extensions run client-side. No servers, no databases, no hosting costs. Edmund's only cost is Mailgun. Jordan's Closet Tools has minimal infrastructure. This means even $3-5K MRR is insanely profitable. The risk: platform dependency (Tony Dinh lost BlackMagic when Twitter changed API pricing).

### Playbook 12: "Template Empires on Existing Platforms"
**Founders:** Thomas Frank, Easlo, Jordan Hughes, Cedric Moore

Build templates for platforms people already use (Notion, Figma, Framer). The platform provides the distribution. Key formula: free templates  build trust  premium templates  bundle sales. Thomas Frank's YouTube-to-template funnel is the gold standard. Easlo's free-to-paid pipeline built 100K+ customers.

### Playbook 13: "The Event/One-Time License Pricing Hack"
**Founders:** Lukas Hermann, Marc Lou (#6), Guilherme Rizzo

If your users don't need your tool continuously, don't force them into monthly subscriptions. Lukas cut churn from 12% to 3% with event licenses. Marc sells ShipFast as one-time. Guilherme sells CSS Scan as lifetime. Match pricing to actual usage patterns.

### Playbook 14: "50 Free SEO Tools in 5 Minutes Each"
**Founders:** Bhanu Teja P, Jon Yongfook (#11), Rashid Khasanov (#15)

Build tiny free tools targeting keywords with difficulty < 10 and 1,000+ monthly searches. Each tool has a CTA back to your paid product. Bhanu builds them in under 5 minutes using Cursor. These 50 tools drive 90% of SiteGPT's traffic. Zero marketing budget needed.

### Playbook 15: "Sponsorware + Screencasts = Developer Revenue"
**Founders:** Caleb Porzio

If you build popular open-source tools, you can monetize through: (1) unedited screencasts behind a GitHub Sponsors paywall, (2) sponsorware (private repos until funding goal met), (3) corporate logo placements on documentation sites. Caleb made $725K from screencasts alone -- 72.5% of his $1M+.

### Playbook 16: "Platform Piggyback (Build ON What's Popular)"
**Founders:** Jordan O'Connor (Poshmark), Edmund Yong (ChatGPT/Claude), Erikas (#9, Shopify), Andy Cloke (#17, Airtable)

Don't build a platform. Build a tool that makes an existing popular platform better. Poshmark has 20M users who need automation. ChatGPT has 200M+ users who need folders. Shopify has millions of merchants who need tracking uploads. The platform IS your distribution.

### Playbook 17: "Directory-as-a-Product"
**Founders:** Piotr Kulpinski

Build a curated directory in a niche  let community submit entries (content flywheel)  monetize through sponsorships and featured listings  extract your codebase into a boilerplate and sell that too. Double revenue from a single codebase. Programmatic SEO creates thousands of ranking pages automatically.

## Combined Key Patterns (SaaS + Non-SaaS)

1. **One-time pricing is underrated.** ShipFast, CSS Scan, TypingMind, DevUtils, Notion templates, Figma kits -- all prove that one-time purchases can generate millions. Not everything needs to be a subscription.

2. **Chrome extensions are money printers.** Near-zero costs, 70-80% margins, built-in distribution via Chrome Web Store. Jordan O'Connor ($756K/yr), CSS Scan ($100K+), Easy Folders ($42K in 6 months), Closet Tools ($2M+ lifetime). The catch: platform dependency risk.

3. **Templates are the new SaaS.** Thomas Frank ($2.1M from Notion templates), Easlo ($600K/yr), Jordan Hughes (six figures from Figma), Cedric Moore ($60K/yr from Framer). No backend, no servers, no support tickets. Pure digital goods with 90%+ margins.

4. **The "engineering as marketing" strategy works.** Build 50 free tiny tools that each target a keyword with difficulty < 10. Each tool has a CTA to your paid product. Bhanu Teja does this in 5 minutes per tool using Cursor. Zero marketing budget, 90% of traffic from these tools.

5. **Platform choice matters more than you think.** Cedric Moore saw a 94% revenue increase just by switching from Gumroad to Lemon Squeezy. Tony Dinh saw 1,300% MRR increase switching to Lemon Squeezy. Checkout UX, abandoned cart emails, and fee structures directly impact revenue.

6. **The sponsorware model is an untapped goldmine for open-source devs.** Caleb Porzio proved you can make $1M+ from GitHub Sponsors alone. The formula: build beloved open-source tools  create unedited screencasts  put behind $14/month paywall  add corporate sponsors. No SaaS billing needed.

7. **Match pricing to usage patterns.** Stagetimer's event licenses (one-time 10-day purchase) cut churn from 12% to 3%. CSS Scan's lifetime license removes subscription fatigue. Don't force monthly billing on users who use your tool sporadically.

8. **Your wife's suggestion might be the best business advice.** Guilherme Rizzo's wife suggested bundle licenses -- drove significant revenue. Jordan O'Connor's wife's Poshmark pain point became a $756K/yr business. Lukas Hermann's wife encouraged him to go full-time. Spouses understand real problems.
